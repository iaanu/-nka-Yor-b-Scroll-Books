The Yoruba Numerical Lexicon — Public Research Release

First Open Scholarly Reference for Yoruba Large-Number Taxonomy

The Ònka Yorùbá Sanctuary is pleased to announce the public release of the Yoruba Numerical Index Database, the first open scholarly reference dedicated to the large-number taxonomy, nomenclature, and computational mapping of an indigenous numerical lexicon. Across every continent, indigenous languages carry centuries of mathematical reasoning, astronomical observation, and numerical logic encoded in counting traditions, trade systems, and oral knowledge. Yet in the modern digital era, these number systems remain largely absent from scientific databases, computational frameworks, and global research infrastructure — leaving a significant gap in how humanity documents and preserves its full intellectual heritage.

This release marks a foundational step toward closing that gap — not only for the Yoruba language, but as a replicable model for indigenous numerical documentation worldwide.

The Yoruba Numerical Index Database provides a standardized framework for naming, scaling, and referencing extremely large numbers within the Yoruba language. It is designed as a template that can be adapted for other indigenous number systems, supporting interdisciplinary use across:

Mathematics and large-number theory
Computer science and algorithm design
Digital humanities and indigenous linguistics
Education, curriculum development, and language revitalization
Artificial intelligence, multilingual computing, and NLP

By establishing consistent nomenclature and index classification, this project demonstrates that indigenous numerical knowledge can — and should — participate fully in modern scientific and technological discourse, to inspire parallel efforts for languages across Africa, the Americas, Asia, the Pacific, and beyond.

Open Data Commitment

This database is released as an open scholarly resource to encourage collaboration, citation, and academic engagement. Libraries, universities, indigenous language organizations, educators, and researchers worldwide are invited to explore, reference, adapt, and build upon this work for their own linguistic communities.

This publication represents Version 1.0 of an evolving global initiative to document and support indigenous numerical systems within contemporary science and technology — proving that no language is too small to count at the highest scales.

📐 Formation Rules

1. Word Joining & Capitalization (PascalCase)

  • Single-Word Rule: When numbers are multiplied to create a new value (e.g., 100 × 3 = 300), they are joined into a single word.
  • Capitalization: Only the first letter of a joined word is capitalized.
    Ọgọ́rǔnlẹ́ta (300)  |  Ẹgbẹ̀rǔnlẹ́fà (6,000)
  • Multi-Word Layout: To express complex numbers: The initial letter of each group of word is capitalized to help distinguish it.
  • Compound Number Arrangement: Numbers are always written from Largest to the Smallest units. For example; (Tredecillions + Quintillions + Quadrillions + Trillions + Billions + Millions + Thousands + Hundreds + Tens + Units).
    Ẹgbẹ̀rǔnOgúnEní = 1,021  |  Ọgọ́rǔnlẹ́sǎnỌgọ́rinẸ́fà = 986

2. Specialized Orthography & Accents

  • The "â" and "ǔ" Marks — Vowel Compression:
    • A single letter may carry two to three accent marks, between the middle, and towards the end of the word. The original tonal expression may be kept short by using the (Up/Down) accent.
    • Every word with two identical vowels at the beginning stays natural without compression (e.g., "Àádọ́rǔn"), this signals speakers to access the words with grace and natural style.
    • Two out of three identical vowels around the middle are being compressed (e.g., "Ẹgbẹ̀rǔnlâádọ́rin"), this signals speakers to continue the words with grace.
    • Every word with two identical vowels towards the end are being compressed (e.g., "Ẹgbẹ̀rǔnlẹ́gbẹ̀rǔn"), this signals speakers to end the words with grace.
